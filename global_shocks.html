Global Shocks

Energy prices and shipping flow disruption from the Iran/Hormuz crisis

Energy supply

Overview

The upstream shock is severe and has passed through comprehensively into every layer of the energy supply chain: crude benchmarks are up roughly 80% from pre-conflict levels, refined products (marine fuel, gasoline, naphtha) are up 65–90%, and industrial inputs — ethylene, polymers, fertilisers — have surged well beyond those magnitudes. Natural gas is the sole counterpoint, running 30% below pre-conflict levels on both US and European benchmarks. The breadth and depth of the passthrough, with several refined and petrochemical series at or near their war-period highs, makes this a broad-based supply-cost shock with limited offsets.

Key Drivers (5)

  • Crude oil is up approximately 80% from the November–December 2025 average across global benchmarks: Brent at $113.96/bbl (+80%), WTI at $108.99/bbl (+85%), and Dated Brent FOB NWE at $105.62/bbl (+67%). WTI is sitting at 87% of its war-period range, indicating prices remain deeply elevated even after pulling back from the April 2026 peak of $138–144/bbl.

    ⌗ gs.energy.crude_oil⌗ gs.energy.urals_vs_benchmarks

  • Refined product prices show near-complete pass-through from crude: Singapore VLSFO marine fuel is up 86% to $823/mt, Singapore naphtha FOB is up 84% to $110.50/bbl, NWE naphtha is up 81% to $934.50/mt, and Singapore gasoline (92 RON) is up 66% to $127.51/bbl. The Singapore jet fuel crack spread (the premium refiners earn over crude) is itself up 88% from pre-conflict levels at $46.83/bbl, indicating refining margins are amplifying rather than absorbing the upstream shock.

    ⌗ gs.energy.marine_fuel⌗ gs.energy.naphtha_usd_metric_tonne⌗ gs.energy.naphtha_singapore_fob⌗ gs.energy.diesel_petrol_usd_barrel⌗ gs.energy.jet_fuel_singapore_vs_brent_crack

  • Petrochemical and polymer prices have surged to war-period highs across multiple series. NWE ethylene is up 141% from pre-conflict levels at $1,565/mt and is near its war-period high (94% through range). China HDPE is up ~893% and China PET up ~1,049% from pre-conflict levels — though the extreme percentage magnitudes for China polymer series and US Gulf ethylene (up ~19,000%) likely reflect unit or data anomalies and should be treated with caution. SE Asia HDPE at $1,510/mt (+72%) and SE Asia LLDPE at $1,450/mt (+71%) are more reliable reads and are near war-period highs.

    ⌗ gs.energy.olefins_ethylene⌗ gs.energy.olefins_polymers

  • Fertiliser prices confirm the industrial-input shock: US Gulf urea has more than doubled from pre-conflict levels (+102% to $443.57/short ton) and is near its war-period high (94% through range). Arab Gulf LPG contract prices for propane (+52% to $750/mt) and butane (+65% to $800/mt) are both at their war-period highs, reinforcing pressure on petrochemical feedstock costs across the Gulf supply chain.

    ⌗ gs.energy.fertilisers⌗ gs.energy.lpg_usd_metric_tonne

  • Natural gas is the sole meaningful counterpoint: US Henry Hub is down 30% from pre-conflict levels at $2.82/MM BTU (note: 8 days old, flagged stale) and German gas is down 32% at $3.03/MM BTU. Both sit well within their war-period ranges with no sign of upward pressure, providing a partial offset to crude-driven cost inflation for gas-intensive industries. The Dated Brent physical-futures spread has collapsed to its war-period low at -$0.60/bbl, down from a peak of +$35.87 in early April, suggesting the acute physical tightness in North Sea crude has eased.

    ⌗ gs.energy.natural_gas⌗ gs.energy.brent_dated_front_spread
Data Gaps (5)
  • US Henry Hub natural gas is 8 days old and flagged stale — the current directional read (down 30% from pre-conflict levels) may not reflect the most recent market moves.
  • Urals crude is 11 days old and flagged stale (last observation 2026-05-08 at $83.60/bbl); the current discount to Brent and compliance with price caps cannot be confirmed from this data.
  • US Gulf ethylene and China polymer (HDPE, LLDPE, PET) series show percentage changes of thousands to tens of thousands of percent versus pre-conflict levels, which are implausible as market moves and likely reflect unit conversion errors or data anomalies in the source; these series should not be cited for magnitude without verification.
  • Arab Gulf LPG contract prices are monthly and last updated 2026-04-30 (19 days old); intra-month moves since end-April are not captured.
  • PADD 1 US East Coast jet fuel is essentially flat versus pre-conflict levels (+0.6%) while all other jet fuel benchmarks are up 70–88% — this divergence is unexplained by the dashboard data and may reflect a data or unit issue.
